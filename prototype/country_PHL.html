Phillipines

The Philippines is a leading producer of nickel (the world’s largest in 2014), gold and copper but also exports iron ore, chromium, zinc and silver and produces oil and gas. Extractive industries accounted for 0.75% of GDP, close to 4% of government revenue and 11.5% of exports in 2014 according to the 2013 PH-EITI Report. Public debates have centred on the environmental and social impacts of mining as well as government taxation of the extractive industries. PH-EITI has undertaken active regional outreach and plans to pilot subnational EITI councils in two provinces in 2016.

Natural Resources

The Philippines has rich deposits of gold, copper, nickel, chromite as well as reserves of coal, zinc, iron, molybdenum, crude oil and natural gas. While the Philippines is ranked as the world’s fifth most mineralised country by estimated reserves, only around 2% of the 9m hectares holding mineral reserves are covered by mining permits. Total reserves of gold, copper and zinc declined between 2010 and 2014, while estimated reserves of nickel and chromite grew significantly.

Tax and Legal Framework

The main taxes levied on the mining sector are corporate income tax, excise tax on minerals and royalties on mineral reservations, while the major oil and gas levies are the government’s share of oil and gas production, corporate income tax and withholding tax on profit remittance to principal. The Bureau of Internal Revenue (BIR) is the main body responsible for collecting taxes paid to central government, while the Mines and Geosciences Bureau of the Department of Environment and Natural Resources and the Department of Energy collect sector levies for mining and coal, oil and gas respectively. Local government units (LGUs) are responsible for collecting subnational payments. Many contracts are disclosed, including 44 published on the PH-EITI contracts portal.

Contracts in Phillipines

The Philippines’ mining, oil and gas contracts

Mining licences are awarded on a “first come first served” basis, while oil and gas petroleum service contracts (PSCs) are concluded through a public competitive tender. The 2012 moratorium on new mining licences was lifted in 2013, with 150 new exploration permit applications received by November 2015. As of 2015 there were 29 active oil and gas PSCs.

Production

The Philippines is a leading producer of mineral commodities such as nickel (the world’s largest producer in 2014), gold (20th) and chromite (17th), but also produces copper, zinc, iron, silver, crude oil and natural gas. Oil and gas production has slumped since 2010, but output of most minerals aside from gold, silver and iron ore has grown. Exploration activities in mining are spread nationwide, while coal production is focused in the province of Antique. Oil and gas exploration is focused offshore.

Government Revenue

The latest EITI disclosures (2013) show that the Philippines received USD 982 million from extractive industry taxation. Almost 90% of these revenues come from oil and gas, with the rest from mining,including coal. Oil and gas revenues were mainly collected through the government’s share of oil and gas production (63% of oil and gas revenues) and corporate income tax (28%), while mining revenues were mainly collected through corporate income tax and excise tax on minerals (each 27% of total mining revenues). Revisions to the Mining Law were proposed in Congress in 2015 and include amended levies.

Distribution

Local government units (LGUs) are entitled to 40% of the total collections from extractive companies in their locality. To facilitate the transfer of LGUs’ shares, the relevant central government agencies issue a certification to the Department of Budget and Management (DBM), which is then tasked to release the shares to the LGUs. From 2016 DBM will now disclose local governments’ share of extractive industry revenues to enable them to see how much they are receiving from each company. DBM will also implement a system of direct transfers of shares from the Bureau of Treasury to the LGUs, effectively speeding up subnational transfers.

Implementation of EITI

The Philippines is planning to launch its 2016 EITI workplan at its National Conference on 12 February 2016. The objectives of the Philippines’ EITI implementation include strengthening national resource management, improving public understanding of the extractive industries and strengthening the business environment. PH-EITI launched a contracts portal in October 2015 and plans to migrate to online EITI reporting in 2017.

Innovations

  • The 2012 Report includes figures on commodity reserves and information on free and prior informed consent (FPIC), environmental funds and detailed explanations of contracts and the policy for contract disclosure.
  • A contracts portal was launched in October 2015, extracting contract information from PDF and allowing more interactive use.
  • The 2013 Report includes two scoping studies, on small-scale metallic mining and large-scale non-metallic mining.
  • The national PH-EITI is planning to pilot subnational EITI councils in two provinces in 2016: South Cotabato and Compostela Valley.

Governance

The Government of the Philippines committed to implement EITI on 6 July 2012, through the Executive Order 79. The 2012 statement on EITI by President Benigno S. Aquino III outlines the reasons for joining the EITI, while the multi-stakeholder statement of commitment to the EITI provides support for establishing the multi-stakeholder group (MSG). Established in January 2013, the MSG’s Terms of Reference establish the roles and responsibilities of MSG members, while the MSG’s internal rules regulates its functioning. The Philippines submitted its EITI Candidature application (and annexes) on 5 April 2013 to the EITI Board. The multi-stakeholder group is the governing body for EITI implementation. It has 16 members, and is chaired by Assistant Secretary of the Department of Finance, Hon. Ma. Teresa S. Habitan. The MSG is supported by a Technical Working Group of 15 members. The government has submitted a draft transparency law to Congress, which would make EITI reporting mandatory for all companies operating in the extractive industries.
